Public roadmaps used to be controversial. The standard objection: "Why give competitors visibility into what we're building?" The newer objection: "What if we miss the date?"

Both objections are real. They're also outweighed, for most SaaS teams, by what a public roadmap actually does: build trust, set expectations, and turn your most engaged users into evangelists. This post is the case for a public roadmap, the case against, and a practical playbook for running one without the common failure modes.

## What a public roadmap actually is

A public roadmap is **not** a project plan with dates. It's a curated, public-facing view of what your team is working on, organized by status, with enough specificity that users believe you and not so much that you box yourself in.

The minimum viable public roadmap has three columns:

1. **Considering / In review** — features you're evaluating but haven't committed to.
2. **Planned / Up next** — features you intend to ship in the foreseeable future.
3. **In progress / Shipping soon** — features actively being built.

Some teams add a fourth column — **Shipped** — but most have moved that to a separate **Changelog** page since it serves a different purpose (announcement vs. plan).

What a public roadmap **doesn't** include:

- Dates or quarters. (More on this below.)
- Internal-only initiatives.
- Features so far out you might not actually build them.
- Strategic moves that would tip off competitors.

## The case for a public roadmap

### 1. It reduces "what are you building?" support volume

Every SaaS team gets the same question on repeat: "When are you adding X?" A public roadmap answers it without anyone touching a keyboard. Support volume drops, sales calls move faster, and prospects can self-serve the question of whether your product fits their needs.

### 2. It makes upgrade decisions easier for prospects

A common pattern in B2B SaaS: a prospect is on the fence because feature X is missing. They book a sales call to ask. Your AE says "it's coming next quarter." They believe it half-heartedly and stall. With a public roadmap, the prospect sees the roadmap themselves, sees feature X in **In progress**, and signs up.

Same conversation, more credibility, less sales-team time.

### 3. It turns your most engaged users into evangelists

Users who voted on a feature **want it to ship**. When it does, they're emotionally invested. They'll tweet about it, tell their teammates, and write LinkedIn posts about how they "love that the team listens." This is real, organic distribution — and it costs you nothing beyond running the roadmap.

### 4. It's a recruiting signal

Engineers who are evaluating your company look at your public roadmap. A good roadmap signals: "this team has a coherent product vision, ships consistently, and is honest with its users." That's a stronger recruiting signal than any "we're building the future of X" landing page.

### 5. It builds accountability inside your team

A public commitment is a stronger commitment. Putting "Slack integration" in the **In progress** column publicly is a forcing function — your team can't quietly deprioritize it without the public ask "what happened to the Slack integration?"

## The case against

### 1. Competitor visibility

Real risk, often overstated. Most competitors aren't reading your roadmap with enough attention to copy it before you ship. The exception: if you're building something genuinely novel that takes 12+ months and a small competitor could rush a thinner version to market in 3, keep it off the roadmap.

### 2. Missed expectations

If you put feature X in **In progress** and don't ship for six months, users get angry. The fix is **never publishing dates** and being conservative about what's "in progress" — but the failure mode is real.

### 3. It creates pressure to ship the wrong things

When users vote heavily on something, there's pressure to ship it even if you've concluded it's not the right move. Discipline matters. The teams that run public roadmaps well are willing to publicly say "we considered this, decided not to ship it, here's why."

### 4. Maintenance overhead

A stale public roadmap is worse than no roadmap. If you put "Slack integration" in **Planned** in January 2025 and it's still there in January 2026, you've broken trust. The cost of a public roadmap is the discipline to update it weekly — minimum.

## The dates problem

The biggest mistake teams make with public roadmaps: putting dates on them.

"Coming Q3 2026." "Shipping by end of year." "Available March 2027." Each of these is an unforced error. Software teams miss dates routinely — that's not a critique, it's the nature of building software with imperfect information. But once you've committed to a date publicly, missing it is a trust event.

The right way to communicate timing on a public roadmap is **status-only**:

- **Considering:** "we're thinking about it, no timeline."
- **Planned:** "we'll work on it before too long."
- **In progress:** "we're actively building it."

That's it. No quarters, no months, no estimates. If a customer asks "when?", your support team can respond with the status — "it's in progress now, no firm ship date yet."

The exception: features that are imminent (next 4 weeks) are fine to date if the team is **very confident**. But default to status-only.

## How to run one without the failure modes

### Use a feedback tool, not a Notion doc

A roadmap maintained in Notion, Trello, or a Google Doc is going to go stale. The best roadmaps are wired into your feedback tool — when you change a feedback post's status from **Planned** to **In progress**, the public roadmap updates automatically.

This also gives you the upvote signal — users who voted on a post get notified when it moves through statuses. Free distribution, no extra work.

### Update on a cadence

Pick a cadence: weekly, biweekly, monthly. Stick to it. Most public roadmaps fail because they go stale, not because they're badly designed.

The minimum cadence: **once per week**, even if it's just a 5-minute scan of "did anything ship this week? did anything start? do statuses match reality?"

### Show "Won't ship" honestly

The hardest column to add to a public roadmap is **Won't ship** or **Considered, declined**. Most teams skip it because saying no in public is uncomfortable.

It's also the highest-trust signal you can send. A roadmap with a visible **Won't ship** column says "we make hard decisions and we tell you about them." Users may disagree with specific decisions, but they trust the system.

The format matters: each declined post should have a one-paragraph "why" attached. Not "we don't think this is important" — that's lazy. Something like: "We considered this seriously and decided that for our scale and audience, the maintenance cost outweighs the benefit. We'll revisit if more teams ask for it."

### Combine with a changelog

Roadmap is **future**. Changelog is **past**. Your shipped-feature page is your changelog, and it should link back to the original feedback post. ("Shipped: Dark mode → originally requested by 47 users in [this post].")

This closes the loop publicly and turns shipping into a marketing event.

### Don't put strategy on it

A public roadmap is a **product** roadmap, not a strategy roadmap. Putting "expand to enterprise" or "explore international markets" on a public roadmap is over-sharing. Strategy decisions belong in board decks, not on a public URL.

The line: if it's a feature your users would experience, it goes on the roadmap. If it's a business move, it doesn't.

### Let users vote, but prioritize on signal

Vote counts on roadmap posts are useful as a signal, not a contract. The team that runs the roadmap should weight votes by:

- Customer segment (paying customers > free users)
- MRR (high-MRR customers > low-MRR customers, where applicable)
- Strategic alignment (does this feature support the direction we're heading?)

Communicate this clearly: "We use upvotes as a signal, but final prioritization is the team's call."

## Examples done well

The public roadmaps we'd hold up as examples:

- **Linear's** — clean, status-only, regularly updated, ties to changelog.
- **Raycast's** — wide visibility, includes "Considered, declined" with explanations.
- **Plausible Analytics'** — open product roadmap on GitHub, full transparency.
- **Vercel's product changelog** — paired with a public roadmap, both are kept religiously up to date.

What they share: status-only timing, regular cadence, willingness to say no.

## Should you run one?

A practical decision tree:

- **You're pre-PMF:** Probably skip it. Your roadmap will change every two weeks. Wait until you have 50+ paying customers and a more stable direction.
- **You're an early-stage SaaS (50-500 customers):** Run a small one. 5-10 items, weekly updates, focus on building trust with the people who already pay you.
- **You're scaling (500+ customers, established product):** Run a comprehensive one. It's a marketing surface, a recruiting signal, and a forcing function for your team. Worth the maintenance overhead.
- **You're enterprise-only:** Probably skip the public version. Run an internal customer-shared roadmap instead, gated behind a customer login.
- **You're a devtool or OSS project:** Almost always yes. Your audience expects transparency.

For most SaaS teams that pass the "we have a product and customers" bar, a public roadmap is positive expected value. The maintenance cost is real but small. The trust upside is real and large.

The teams that run public roadmaps badly cause themselves real damage — stale roadmaps and missed dates erode trust faster than no roadmap at all. The teams that run them well turn their most engaged users into a distribution channel.
